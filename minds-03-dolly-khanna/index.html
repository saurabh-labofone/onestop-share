So why couldn't the big funds just buy what she bought?
1
Too small to matter. A fund running thousands of crores can't take a real position in a ₹few-hundred-crore micro-cap without moving the price — or breaching its own limits.
2
Nobody covers it. No analyst writes research on shrimp feed, pressure cookers or sugar. No report means no approval to buy.
3
Career risk. A famous stock dips — "the market's fault." An unknown micro-cap dips — you're the fund manager who's fired.
She fished in the one pond institutions are structurally barred from.
Why big investors avoid micro-caps: liquidity / impact cost, single-stock and concentration limits, and no sell-side coverage of unglamorous sectors. A structural blind spot, not a lack of insight.

Caption — Instagram post body

Everyone knows the legend: a Chennai homemaker who quietly out-picked the institutions. It's a great story. It's also half a myth.

The research behind "Dolly Khanna" is run by her husband Rajiv, an IIT-Madras chemical engineer. The seed money came from selling a frozen-foods business to Hindustan Unilever in 1995. The disclosed holdings peaked near ₹584 crore. All real. The "lone housewife genius" framing? That's the part the headlines added.

What's actually worth copying isn't the persona. It's where they looked. In 2014 they bought a then-ignored shrimp-feed company — roughly 3% of the portfolio, the kind of small-cap no fund manager wanted to defend in a meeting. By the time the "Dolly Khanna bought it" headlines hit, the quiet entry was already gone.

Here's the trap most retail investors fall into. You only see a stock once it crosses the 1% disclosure line — after it's already worked. The small losers and early exits never show up. So the track record looks cleaner than it was, and the people who pile in on the news inherit the volatility without the cost basis. Same illiquid small-caps that gave 5x on the way up can gut a portfolio on the way down.

The lesson isn't "find the next Avanti." It's: copying someone's holdings is not the same as copying their edge. Their edge was being early and quiet. By the time you read about it, you're late and loud.

Save this for the next time a "star investor's portfolio" headline tempts you. Then go read an annual report nobody's talking about.
