Caption — Instagram post body

Benjamin Graham wrote the rulebook. "Never put more than ~5% in one stock." "Buy the statistically cheap." "Diversify." Every value investor alive still quotes him.

Then in 1948 he put about a fifth to a quarter of his fund into one insurer — GEICO — at 4–5x his own limit. It became the best trade of his life by a mile. In the back of The Intelligent Investor he admitted the GEICO profit alone "far exceeded the sum of all the others" from twenty years of careful, diversified work. And then he asked the most honest question any investor has ever printed: one lucky break, or one supremely shrewd decision — can we tell them apart?

That's the part most people skip. They memorize his rules and never notice his results came from the one trade that broke them. His own student, Buffett, took the heresy further — dropping Graham's cigar-butt cheapness for "a wonderful company at a fair price" (Munger's line, not Graham's) — and bought GEICO outright.

The lesson isn't "concentrate" or "diversify." It's that the man who built the discipline was humble enough to mark his own scorecard, name the luck, and not pretend he had it all figured out. Most of us can't admit that about a single trade.

Markets will do what they do. Your job is to be honest about what worked, what was luck, and what you'd do again. That's harder than picking stocks.
