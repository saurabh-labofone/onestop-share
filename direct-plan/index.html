Caption — Instagram post body

A stranger makes money off your mutual fund. Every year. It's completely legal — and you'll never spot it on your statement.

Here's the secret: every fund has a twin. Same fund, same manager, same returns — but two versions. Direct (just the fund) and Regular (the fund + a passenger). Almost nobody knows the cheaper twin exists.

The passenger is the person who sold it to you. If you'd picked Direct, he earns ₹0 — so he put you on Regular, where he earns a cut every year. That's why he never mentioned the cheaper one. (The Regular−Direct price gap is literally his trail commission.)

And what did he do to earn it? Filled one form, once. On a ₹10,000/month SIP over 25 years at 12%, a ~1% Regular-vs-Direct gap is the difference between roughly ₹1.88 crore (Direct) and ₹1.58 crore (Regular) — about ₹30 lakh. That's the same ₹30 lakh you put in over those years. Every rupee you saved, he matched, from your pocket. Why so much? Because he doesn't take 1% once — he takes 1% of a pot that keeps growing, so his cut compounds too.

The annoying part: most people never chose Regular. It was the default. Not your fault — but it's your move now. Switch each fund to Direct (via MFCentral or directly with the AMC/RTA): same fund, no passenger, 10 minutes. When you actually need advice, pay a SEBI-registered investment adviser a flat fee — not a cut of everything you own. (Switching old units can trigger exit load or capital-gains tax, so check that once; for new SIPs, just pick Direct.)
