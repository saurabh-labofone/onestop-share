Caption — Instagram post body

Warren Buffett beat the market for 60 years — mostly by sitting still. From 1965 to 2023, Berkshire Hathaway compounded at 19.8% a year while the S&P 500 did 10.2%. That gap sounds small. It isn't.

$1,000 invested with Buffett in 1965 became roughly $44 million. The same $1,000 in the S&P 500: about $313,000. Same start, same six decades — about 140 times the money, for waiting.

His clearest example: he bought Coca-Cola in 1988, built a ~$1.3 billion stake, and has never sold a single share. The dividend alone now hands Berkshire back its entire original cost every year and a half or so. As he wrote in 1990: "Lethargy bordering on sloth remains the cornerstone of our investment style."

Everyone quotes "be greedy when others are fearful." The harder rule is the boring one: most days, the right move is no move. He reads five to six hours a day — mostly to decide to do nothing. Investing isn't really about making good decisions; it's about not making bad ones when you're bored. Your portfolio doesn't need more of your attention. It needs less.

So: how long do you take to decide to buy? How long to sell? And why is one so much faster than the other?
